Why I Ditched Windows, Burned the ISO, and Entered the Linux Cult

"They told me Linux was for hackers and weirdos. They were right. So I installed it."

I don't know when it happened. Maybe it was the 38th unwanted Windows update or the way Edge forced itself into my startup like a clingy ex. But something in me snapped.

I needed out.
Out of bloat.
Out of telemetry.
Out of the Big Tech zoo.

The First Taste: Linux Mint Cinnamon

So I dipped my toes into the shallow end: Linux Mint Cinnamon. Friendly UI. Stable. Felt like Ubuntu but without the annoying pop-ups and pretentious energy.

But then‚Ä¶
I saw it.
A riced Arch desktop on r/unixporn.

It was over.

Enter the Void: Ricing and the Terminal Cult

No one tells you what ricing really means. It's not just "customizing your desktop."
It's philosophy. It's minimalism mixed with madness. It's staring at your i3 config file for 2 hours just to make your volume keys work.

I went from GUI baby mode to living in the terminal. File managers? I am the file manager.

Bash became my prayer.
Neovim, my scroll.
And when I booted into a black screen with no cursor after updating GRUB?
That was my baptism.

Why I Stayed

  • My system only runs what I install.
  • No forced updates, no spyware, no bullsh*t.
  • I started understanding how operating systems work ‚Äî not just clicking buttons.

Most of all? It felt like mine.
I wasn't just using tech. I was co-creating it.

For Devs Thinking of Switching

Do it. Break things. Reinstall 5 times. Cry. RTFM. Rise.
It'll make you a better developer, guaranteed. You'll learn networking, scripting, file structures, permission systems ‚Äî all by necessity. No more "dev mode." Just real mode.

Last words:

"Linux didn't just change my OS. It gave me back control. In a world of autoplay, that's priceless."
